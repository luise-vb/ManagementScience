Project 03 / Decision Analysis

Decision Trees

Structuring uncertain business decisions through expected value analysis and probability-weighted payoffs

01 — Overview

Making Decisions Under Uncertainty

Decision trees are a structured framework for evaluating choices when outcomes are uncertain. By mapping out decision nodes (choices), chance nodes (probabilities), and payoffs, managers can calculate the Expected Value (EV) of each path and select the highest-value option.

In the real world, decision trees appear in product launch decisions (launch or not?), marketing strategy (predict whether a customer will buy), investment analysis, and clinical trials. They force explicit thinking about probabilities and consequences — making assumptions visible.

03 — Evidence from My Work

Applications & Limitations

From my class notes on decision trees:

Real-world applications I identified:

✓ Application

Business launch decisions — structure the choice of whether to bring a new product to market based on market research probabilities.

✓ Application

Marketing & consumer behaviour — predict whether a customer will purchase, using historical probability data to weight expected revenue.

Limitations I noted:

⚠ Limitation

Oversimplification of reality — real decisions involve more branches and dependencies than a single tree can cleanly represent.

⚠ Limitation

Heavy dependence on assumptions — the accuracy of any decision tree output is only as good as the probability estimates used.

⚠ Limitation

Omitted factors — intangibles like employee morale, brand reputation, and strategic fit are difficult to assign monetary values.

⚠ Limitation

Probability accuracy — how do we know our estimated probabilities are correct? Garbage-in, garbage-out applies here.

My open questions from class:

  • ? How do we know the probabilities are accurate — and how sensitive is the decision to small changes in those estimates?
  • ? When should I use a decision tree instead of other methods — like simulation, scoring models, or intuitive judgement?
04 — What Did I Learn?

Knowledge, Skills & Reflection

Knowledge

Decision trees decompose complex choices into explicit branches. Each chance node multiplies a payoff by its probability; rolling back from right to left yields the best expected value at each decision point.

Skills

I practised mapping decisions onto tree structures, computing expected values, and identifying which branches to prune. I also learned to question probability assumptions before trusting an output.

Reflection

The biggest insight: the method doesn't remove uncertainty — it makes uncertainty explicit and manageable. Its value lies in forcing structured thinking, even when the probabilities themselves are uncertain.
