Project 01 / Reading & Film Analysis

Star Wars: A New Hope

How a galaxy far, far away illustrates the core concepts of management science

01 — Overview

Why We Watched Star Wars

In class we were asked to watch Star Wars: A New Hope (1977) through the lens of management science — not as a sci-fi adventure, but as a case study in decision-making, resource allocation, operations, and organisational behaviour under extreme constraint.

The film turns out to be a surprisingly rich source of management science examples. The Rebel Alliance operates with scarce resources against a vastly better-resourced opponent. The Empire demonstrates the dangers of over-centralised command. Every major plot point can be mapped onto a concept from the course — from network optimisation to decision trees to supply chain failure.

"Do, or do not. There is no try." Yoda — also a reasonable summary of binary decision analysis
02 — Demonstration

Scene-by-Scene Analysis

Select a scene to explore how it connects to a specific management science concept.

Scene Explorer

The Scene
The Rebel Alliance receives the Death Star blueprints and must plan a surgical strike against a single two-metre exhaust port. Leadership weighs attack routes, pilot assignments, and probability of success — with no second attempt possible.
Decision Analysis
Management Science Connection
This is a classic decision tree under uncertainty. Two branches: attack the exhaust port (high risk, catastrophic upside) or attempt an alternative strategy (lower risk, no guaranteed outcome). The Rebels implicitly calculate expected value — the probability of success times the payoff (destroying the Death Star) versus the cost of failure. They choose the high-variance option because the alternative is guaranteed defeat.
The Scene
Luke, Wedge, and Biggs fly down the Death Star trench in sequence. Two pilots fail their runs before Luke reaches the target. TIE fighters pursue; Vader closes in. Han Solo's last-minute arrival eliminates the constraint — clearing the path for Luke's shot.
Network Optimisation
Management Science Connection
The trench run is a network routing problem. The "nodes" are pilot entry → trench segment → exhaust port target. Each segment has associated risk (TIE fighters = cost). The Rebels send pilots sequentially — effectively testing routes until one succeeds. Han Solo's intervention is a constraint removal: eliminating Vader from the "network" drops the cost of Luke's path from near-infinite to feasible. This mirrors how removing a bottleneck node can transform an impassable route.
The Scene
Han Solo is offered payment to fly the Millennium Falcon to Alderaan. He demands 17,000 credits — enough to pay off Jabba the Hutt. He initially refuses to join the Battle of Yavin (no reward), then returns at the last moment. His reversal changes the outcome of the entire mission.
Decision Trees · Incentives
Management Science Connection
Han's arc is a two-stage decision tree with a changing payoff structure. Initially he evaluates: join the battle (risk of death, uncertain reward) vs. leave (certain payment, no risk). His rational choice is to leave. When new information arrives — loyalty, moral stakes — the non-monetary payoff shifts his expected value calculation. This illustrates a key limitation of purely quantitative decision models: they struggle to incorporate values, relationships, and reputation that real decision-makers weigh heavily.
The Scene
Princess Leia loads the Death Star technical readouts into R2-D2. The plans travel from Scarif → R2-D2 → Tatooine → Obi-Wan → Yavin IV. Each transfer point risks interception. The Empire deploys the entire Imperial fleet to prevent delivery.
Supply Chain · Information Flow
Management Science Connection
The movement of the Death Star plans is a supply chain and information network problem. The "product" is critical intelligence; the "supply chain" spans multiple nodes (people, droids, planets) each with failure risk. The Empire's response — deploying maximum force to intercept — mirrors a disruption response strategy: identify the critical path and attack its most vulnerable node. Leia's redundancy approach (encoding in a droid, separating from the main fleet) is exactly what supply chain resilience theory recommends: multiple routing options for critical information.
The Scene
The Rebel Alliance operates from a hidden base on Yavin IV with a handful of X-Wings, Y-Wings, and a limited pilot roster. Against the Death Star — a moon-sized battle station with near-unlimited resources — they must win with what they have. No resupply. No reinforcements.
Resource Allocation · Linear Programming
Management Science Connection
The Rebel Alliance faces a constrained resource allocation problem that is essentially linear programming in practice. Inputs: fixed number of pilots (x₁) and ships (x₂), each with different capabilities and survival probabilities. Objective: maximise the probability of one pilot reaching the exhaust port. Constraints: TIE fighter attrition, time (the Death Star enters firing range), and pilot skill. The briefing scene is literally a corner-point analysis — who flies which route, in what order, to maximise mission success under the tightest possible constraints.
03 — Evidence from My Work

Concept Mapping

The film maps onto six distinct management science concepts covered in this course. The same analytical tools we apply to toy factories and food trucks apply — with surprising fidelity — to a galactic civil war.

1
Film Element
Rebel mission planning (Yavin IV briefing)
Alliance leadership assesses routes, assigns pilots, and evaluates success probability against an overwhelming enemy with a hard deadline.
Management Science Concept
Decision Analysis / Decision Trees
Choosing under uncertainty with explicit payoffs and probabilities. The Rebels pick the highest expected-value option even with a low success probability — because the alternative has zero value.
2
Film Element
The Death Star's single point of failure
Despite immense resources, the Empire's superweapon is destroyed by one unshielded exhaust port — a flaw identified through stolen technical plans.
Management Science Concept
Oversimplification & Model Limitations
Mirrors the key limitation of decision models: heavy dependence on assumptions. The Empire assumed no small fighter could threaten the port. One flawed assumption in a model can make the entire analysis worthless.
3
Film Element
Trench run pilot sequencing
Three pilots attempt the trench run in sequence. Each run depletes TIE fighter attention; Han Solo eliminates Vader as a blocking constraint just before Luke fires.
Management Science Concept
Network Optimisation / Sequencing
Route selection through a constrained network with dynamic costs. Removing a key bottleneck (Vader) changes the minimum-cost path — identical to how clearing a node in a TSP network opens new optimal routes.
4
Film Element
Grand Moff Tarkin's command structure
Tarkin dismisses Vader's concerns about the Force and overrides strategic input from subordinates. All decisions flow through one authority with no redundancy.
Management Science Concept
Centralised Decision-Making Risk
Over-centralised command is a classic organisational failure mode. It excludes qualitative information (Vader's instinct about Luke) that quantitative models cannot capture — exactly the "morale and reputation" limitation of decision trees noted in class.
5
Film Element
R2-D2 carrying the Death Star plans
Critical intelligence is routed through a redundant, low-profile carrier across multiple hand-off points from Scarif to Yavin IV.
Management Science Concept
Supply Chain Resilience
Multi-node information transfer with disruption risk at each handoff. Leia's decision to use R2-D2 as a separate routing channel is a resilience strategy — maintaining parallel paths for critical data mirrors real logistics redundancy planning.
6
Film Element
Han Solo turning back at Yavin
Han initially leaves (rational self-interest). He returns after weighing non-monetary factors — loyalty, purpose, and the moral cost of inaction.
Management Science Concept
Model Limitations — Unmeasurable Factors
Quantitative models cannot capture morale, loyalty, or brand reputation. Han's reversal perfectly illustrates the key limitation of decision trees discussed in class: real decisions include factors that resist monetisation.
Evidence: film viewed in class as assigned course material. Analysis based on in-class discussion of how Star Wars: A New Hope demonstrates decision-making under uncertainty, network constraints, resource allocation, and the limits of purely quantitative models — all core themes of the management science curriculum.
04 — What Did I Learn?

Knowledge, Skills & Reflection

Knowledge

Management science concepts are not abstract — they describe real decision structures that appear everywhere, including fiction. Scarcity, constraint, sequencing, and uncertainty are universal problems regardless of context.

Skills

I practised translating a narrative into an analytical framework: identifying decision nodes, mapping resource constraints, and spotting where qualitative factors override quantitative models — a skill that matters as much in real business as in film analysis.

Reflection

The most surprising insight: the Empire loses not because of poor resources, but poor decision modelling. They built a perfect weapon with one catastrophic assumption error. Every model has its Achilles' heel — always question your inputs before you trust your output.
