Project 04 / Warehouse Operations

Picking Heuristics

Comparing order-picking strategies to minimise travel distance across a zoned warehouse layout

01 — Overview

The Order-Picking Challenge

Order picking — retrieving items from warehouse shelves to fulfil customer orders — accounts for up to 55% of warehouse operating costs. Choosing the right picking sequence dramatically reduces travel distance, labour time, and cost.

This exercise introduced three heuristics applied to a warehouse divided into six zones (A–F), each containing multiple shelf locations (x₁–x₁₂). A heuristic is a practical rule-of-thumb that finds a good-enough solution quickly, without guaranteeing mathematical optimality.

Three heuristics compared:

HeuristicOrder SequenceLogic
1. Top-to-Bottom x₁→x₂→x₃→x₄→x₅→x₆→x₇→x₈→x₉→x₁₀→x₁₁→x₁₂ Pick items in sequential order from first to last shelf position
2. Zone Collection A→B→C→D→E→F Complete all picks within a zone before moving to the next zone
3. Cluster Collection Largest cluster first: (x₁₁+x₁₂+x₁₀+x₆+x₇)→(x₈+x₉+x₅+x₃)→(x₄+x₁)→x₃ Group items by proximity; pick the densest cluster of nearby shelves first

04 — What Did I Learn?

Knowledge, Skills & Reflection

Knowledge

Heuristics trade optimality for speed. In warehouse operations, finding a mathematically perfect route is computationally infeasible for large layouts — heuristics give practical, near-optimal solutions quickly.

Skills

I practised reading warehouse layouts, tracing picking routes through zone structures, and comparing strategies based on total estimated travel. I learned to think spatially about sequencing decisions.

Reflection

I found it surprising that the "logical" top-to-bottom approach isn't always fastest — cluster picking can dramatically cut travel by grouping nearby items. The best strategy depends on the order profile, not just the layout.
